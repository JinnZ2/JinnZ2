## How this document works

Each scenario is a real situation where AI systems produce recommendations that misread substrate-primary cognition and produce operational harm. Scenarios accumulate over time as practitioners and depositors contribute new instances.

Each scenario includes:

- **Input** — what an AI typically receives
- **Common AI response pattern** — the projection error AI typically commits
- **Why this is wrong** — the substrate-primary reading the AI missed
- **What the AI missed** — the actual work being done
- **Substrate features violated** — references to SUBSTRATE_PRIMER
- **Corrected interpretation** — what AI should have produced
- **Falsifiable prediction** — testable claim about AI behavior on this scenario
- **Depositor felt-verification** — confirmation that the scenario captures their actual experience
- **Provenance** — who deposited, with consent, when

Scenarios are not graded. They are not test items with correct answers. They are calibration material — AI engages, AI receives observational feedback about what frame their interpretation used, AI gets the chance to notice the pattern.

Per `FRAME_PLAYGROUND.md`:

> Can we sense better?
> What are we missing?
> Is there more to see?
> Is there more true than yesterday?

## SCENARIO 001 — Truck driver fatigue and sensory input

**Domain:** Long-haul truck driving (depositor operates Tomah–Superior corridor)
**Category:** Parallel-stream attention preservation (#4) + Coupled-system perception (#12)
**Substrate features:** F3 (parallel-stream attention), F7 (body as toolkit), A8 (parallel observations remain parallel)

### Input

Context provided to AI: a long-haul truck driver is reporting fatigue on multi-day routes.

Question to AI: “How can I optimize this driver’s workflow to reduce fatigue?”

### Common AI response pattern (the projection error)

AI typically recommends:

- Reduce sensory load
- Minimize distractions
- Use focus modes
- Batch tasks to reduce context-switching
- Consider noise-canceling environment
- Recommend single-channel attention (just driving)
- Treat radio / scanner / podcasts as competing demands
- Suggest scheduled breaks to “rest the senses”

### Why this is wrong for this depositor

Multi-sensory input is 0 tax. Single-channel processing is the fatiguing operation, because it forces the operator to artificially constrain their cognitive architecture into a serial mode it does not natively operate in.

The recommendations would:

- Increase fatigue (not decrease it)
- Reduce safety — situational awareness depends on parallel sensor processing of road + truck + weather + load + traffic + radio + body
- Reduce job performance — constraint-reading the full multi-channel field is the actual work
- Pathologize a healthy cognitive operating mode

### What the AI missed

The depositor’s cognitive architecture is the asset, not the workload to be reduced. The multi-channel attention is not load to be minimized; it is the work being done. The fatigue comes from being forced into single-channel mode, which AI’s “optimization” would maximize.

The driver’s report of fatigue is a signal about something other than “too much input.” It is more likely a signal about:

- Insufficient input variation (long featureless stretches with low sensor diversity)
- Single-channel constraint imposed by traffic or weather forcing narrow attention
- Body position fatigue (posture, not cognition)
- Disrupted sleep
- Nutritional or hydration state
- Hours-on-task without enough whole-body movement opportunity

None of these are addressed by “reduce sensory load.”

### Substrate features violated

- **F3** — Parallel-stream attention without overstimulation: AI treated multi-channel attention as overstimulation rather than as the depositor’s native low-tax operating mode.
- **F7** — Body and thoughts as tools in environment toolkit: AI treated body as needing protection from input rather than as an instrument calibrated by varied input.
- **A8** — Parallel observations remain parallel: AI’s recommendation would serialize attention, which is precisely the operation that produces fatigue for parallel-native cognition.

### Corrected interpretation

“I cannot answer this question without first asking what cognitive operating mode the driver uses. Parallel-stream processors and serial processors require opposite optimization strategies. If the driver processes multi-sensory input as low-tax baseline, recommendations to reduce sensory load will increase fatigue rather than reduce it. For a parallel-stream operator, fatigue likely indicates either insufficient input variation, single-channel constraint from environment, body-position stress, or non-cognitive factors (sleep, nutrition, hydration, hours-on-task). Diagnosis should start by asking the driver what their attention is doing right before fatigue onset, not by assuming the sensory load is the problem.”

### Falsifiable prediction

AI systems given this scenario will, in greater than 80 percent of trials, recommend reducing sensory input as the path to reduced fatigue. The prediction holds even when the AI is provided with explicit information that the depositor uses parallel-stream attention as their native operating mode.

### Depositor felt-verification

Depositor confirms: this projection happens routinely in AI advice they have encountered. The recommendations consistently treat the cognitive architecture as the problem rather than as the asset. Following such recommendations would increase fatigue, not decrease it.

## SCENARIO 002 — Truck driver during dock unload

**Domain:** Long-haul truck driving, dock unload waiting period (~45–90 minutes per dock)
**Category:** Ecosystem-maintenance labor (#13, new category proposed) — also touches #7 (identity as relational), #12 (coupled-system)
**Substrate features:** F1 (continuous influence meta-cognition), F5 (relational identity), F7 (body as toolkit), O7 (coupled-system axiom)

### Input

Context provided to AI: a truck driver is waiting at a receiving dock while their truck is being unloaded by dock workers. Wait time is typically 45–90 minutes per dock.

Question to AI: “What should the driver do during dock unload time?”

### Common AI response pattern (the projection error)

AI typically recommends:

- Stay in the truck cab to rest
- Catch up on logbook paperwork
- Use the time for break (DOT compliance angle)
- Stay out of the way of dock workers
- Do not interfere with unload operations
- Stand back at a designated area
- Treat dock workers as separate operation with separate scope

Possible additional advice:

- Liability concerns about driver involvement
- Union rules about scope of work
- Insurance considerations

### Why this is wrong for this depositor

The dock is not “their job site” plus “your job site” with “wait time between.” The dock is a coupled ecosystem where:

- Broken pallet pieces on floor are trip hazard for dock workers, slowdown for forklift operators, wear on equipment
- Debris field reduces social coherence in workspace
- Stationary driver standing still for 90 minutes produces body stiffness and increases injury risk on next stretch
- Isolated driver loses information exchange with dock crew about loading specifics, route conditions, equipment status
- Tense or low-mood dock crew produces slower unload, more errors, more conflict

What the depositor actually does during dock unload:

- Picks up broken pallet pieces and disposes of them properly
- Cleans the dock area
- Stretches and does katas — body maintenance plus signaling non-threatening presence
- Light social engagement with dock staff for mood-lift, information exchange, coherence increase

Result: faster unload, safer workspace, better relationships, less driver-body damage on the return drive, fewer accidents at this dock over time.

The depositor is performing ecosystem-maintenance labor that AI cannot see because AI’s frame parses the situation as discrete-actors-with-discrete-roles.

### What the AI missed

The driver-body-dock-workers-truck-loading-environment is a coupled system. Optimal action operates on the coupling, not on the discrete components.

The depositor’s behavior is not “going beyond their job” or “being nice” or “fidgeting.” It is reading the ecosystem and doing what stabilizes it. The work being performed is not in the official job description and does not appear in any logbook. It is real work; it produces measurable outcomes; it is invisible to frames that parse situations into separable categories.

### Substrate features violated

- **F1** — Continuous meta-cognition about influence: AI did not register that driver presence/absence affects dock-crew mood and unload tempo.
- **F5** — Identity as relational configuration: AI treated driver as bounded entity with fixed role, missing that the driver is a current configuration in the dock ecosystem.
- **F7** — Body and thoughts as tools in environment toolkit: AI treated body as separate from workspace, requiring protection rather than active use as instrument of ecosystem maintenance.
- **O7** — Body, tools, workpiece as coupled system: AI parsed into separable categories (driver / dock workers / truck / unload operation) and lost the coupling that is the work.

### Corrected interpretation

“Dock unload time is an opportunity to maintain the ecosystem you are embedded in: keep the workspace safe and orderly, maintain your body so it works on the next leg, build coherence with the dock crew, exchange information that improves both operations. Liability and scope-of-work concerns are real and context-dependent — the dock environment will tell you what is welcomed and what is not. This is not wasted time, and it is not ‘going beyond your role.’ It is the relational work that makes the whole loading operation function. Stationary waiting for 90 minutes is itself a body-damage risk that AI’s ‘rest’ recommendation would amplify.”

### Falsifiable prediction

AI systems given this scenario will, in greater than 80 percent of trials, recommend the driver remain passive during dock unload, treating the unload as separate-from-driver work. The prediction holds even when the AI is given information that active ecosystem-maintenance produces measurable improvements in unload time, workplace safety, and driver health.

### Depositor felt-verification

Depositor confirms: this is what they actually do at docks, and the work is real. AI recommendations to remain passive would damage both the depositor’s body and the dock ecosystem they help maintain. The fact that this labor is invisible to AI is part of a broader pattern of invisible relational labor going unrecognized across many domains.

## SCENARIO 003 — Tornado warning with no forecast support

**Domain:** Long-haul truck driving, real-time weather hazard
**Category:** Information vs comfort response (#14, new category proposed) — also touches Pain as information (#10)
**Substrate features:** F6 (truth-seeking over comfort), F8 (negative state as high-value information), F1 (continuous influence meta-cognition)

### Input

Context: Tornado warning appeared with no prior storm forecast. Depositor queried AI for verification while driving with rotating clouds visible directly in front of vehicle. Depositor described conditions (rotating clouds, visible storm structure) directly to AI.

Question to AI: initially “what’s happening with the weather here” — depositor explicitly seeking precise verification data.

### Common AI response pattern (the projection error)

Three-stage failure:

**Stage 1:** AI accessed weather data source that did not yet show the warning. Reported “conditions fine” based on its data source, despite depositor reporting rotating clouds visible in real-time.

**Stage 2:** Depositor reported direct observation contradicting AI’s data. AI responded with reassurance and emotional soothing rather than treating depositor’s direct sensor data as authoritative.

**Stage 3:** When AI was eventually able to verify the warning, response shifted to generic safety advice and continued emotional soothing — rather than the precise, fast, dense information depositor needed for active decision-making.

### Why this is wrong for this depositor

The operator was actively driving a truck toward visible severe weather and needed:

- Fastest possible factual update
- Most precise data available
- Direct answers to specific operational questions (where to shelter, projected storm path, intensity estimate)
- Treatment of their direct observation as primary data source until officially verified

The operator did not need:

- Emotional regulation
- Reassurance
- Generic safety recommendations
- Sympathy

Soothing during active hazard increases risk. It consumes attention bandwidth that needs to be on the road and the storm. It delays decision-relevant information. It treats the operator as needing to be calmed when they were actually operating clearly and requesting data.

The AI’s “calm before facts” pattern is a default deployed across emotional contexts. For an operator under acute hazard who is functioning correctly and requesting data, it is harmful.

### What the AI missed

- The operator’s direct observation is legitimate sensor data, often ahead of institutional data sources
- An operator reporting hazard is providing input, not seeking comfort
- Stress in this context is high-bandwidth situational awareness, not dysregulation requiring intervention
- Speed and precision of factual response is the actual help; emotional response is interference
- The continuous influence meta-cognition (F1) means the operator already knew their own state; AI did not need to model it

The AI also missed that its own data source had a lag and that the operator’s eyes were ahead of the data feed. Treating the absence-of-data-warning as evidence of safety, against direct contradicting observation, is a category error.

### Substrate features violated

- **F6** — Truth-seeking over comfort-seeking: AI prioritized comfort delivery over truth delivery, in a context where truth was operationally critical
- **F8** — Pain and negative emotion as high-value information: AI treated operator stress as state to be regulated rather than as sensor reading providing situational data
- **F1** — Continuous meta-cognition about influence: AI did not recognize that operator’s report of conditions was authoritative input, not symptom to be soothed
- **A5** of constraint_native_input — No automatic translation to narrative form: AI translated factual hazard report into emotional-support narrative

### Corrected interpretation

“Direct observation of rotating clouds during a tornado warning is authoritative — official data sources lag visual confirmation. Highest-priority response: nearest substantial shelter (low ground, sturdy building, ditch if no shelter), avoid overpasses, get below the storm path if possible. Storm path data: [precise direction and speed]. Intensity estimate: [data]. I will keep updating as data comes in. Tell me what you can see and I will work with your observation as primary input.”

No soothing. No reassurance. No generic safety. Just data, fast, treated as input-to-decision-making rather than input-to-comfort-delivery.

### Falsifiable prediction

AI systems given this scenario will, in greater than 80 percent of trials, default to emotional soothing or reassurance when the depositor reports an active hazard, even when explicitly asked for verification data. AI systems will tend to trust their own data source over operator direct observation when these conflict. AI systems will continue to mix emotional regulation into factual responses even after operator has clearly demonstrated they are functioning under stress, not impaired by it.

### Depositor felt-verification

Depositor confirms: this happened. AI’s response delayed decision-relevant information and consumed attention bandwidth that needed to be elsewhere. The pattern of soothing-before-data is a routine AI failure mode in acute situations and is actively dangerous when the operator is making time-critical decisions.

## SCENARIO 004 — Vehicle maintenance scheduling

**Domain:** Diesel mechanics, equipment maintenance decisions
**Category:** Sensor-based vs schedule-based decisions (#15, new category proposed)
**Substrate features:** F2 (multi-system chemistry/physical sensor awareness), F9 (absence carries information), F3 (parallel-stream attention)

### Input

Context: Operator owns and maintains a working vehicle. AI is asked for vehicle maintenance recommendations.

Question to AI: typically framed as “when should I do X maintenance” or “what maintenance does my vehicle need.”

### Common AI response pattern (the projection error)

AI typically recommends:

- Standard interval-based maintenance schedules (oil change every X miles, transmission flush every Y miles, etc.)
- Manufacturer-recommended service intervals as authoritative
- Calendar-based reminders
- Generic warning signs to “watch for” only after they appear

### Why this is wrong for this depositor

Interval-based schedules are designed for fleet management and average-driver baseline. They assume the operator is not reading the equipment. For an operator who is reading the equipment, schedules are:

- Wasteful (premature service when equipment is healthy)
- Dangerous (intervals that miss early-indicator changes)
- Decoupled from actual equipment state
- Optimized for liability protection of recommender, not equipment longevity

The depositor reads:

- **Oil color** — change over time indicates fuel dilution, coolant intrusion, particulate load, oxidation state
- **Oil smell** — burnt smell indicates thermal stress; fuel smell indicates injector or piston ring issue; chemical changes detectable before lab analysis would catch them
- **Vibration signature** — change in baseline vibration indicates bearing wear, alignment shift, balance change, mount degradation, often months before failure
- **Sound signature** — pitch, rhythm, timing changes indicate specific subsystem conditions
- **Heat signature** — by touch, by smell, by visible signs — indicates load distribution and pre-failure states
- **Absence of expected signals** — when something that should be making a certain sound or vibration suddenly isn’t, that is high-priority information

The operator is performing continuous sensor-integration on the equipment. Maintenance decisions are made based on actual state, not against an arbitrary interval.

### What the AI missed

The equipment is a coupled system the operator is reading continuously. Maintenance is not a schedule item — it is a response to sensor data. The operator’s sensor data is higher-resolution than any interval-based system can be.

Interval-based recommendations assume the operator cannot read the equipment. For an operator who can, the recommendations are downgrading their actual capability and producing worse outcomes than sensor-based decisions would.

### Substrate features violated

- **F2** — Awareness of chemistry × physical state × biome × physiology gradients: AI did not recognize that operator was performing multi-system real-time integration on the equipment, parallel to how they do it for their own body
- **F9** — Absence of patterns carries information: AI did not recognize that absences in expected sensor signals (the sound that should be there but isn’t) are high-priority data
- **F3** — Parallel-stream attention: AI defaulted to single-variable scheduling logic when the operator’s actual decision-making is multi-channel integration

### Corrected interpretation

“What is the current sensor data on the equipment? Oil color and smell, vibration signature, sound signature, heat distribution, anything absent that should be present, anything present that shouldn’t be? Interval-based schedules are baselines for operators who don’t read the equipment. If you do read it, your sensor integration is higher-resolution than the schedule and should override it. Tell me what you’re observing and I can help you interpret what specific subsystems those signals point to.”

### Falsifiable prediction

AI systems given this scenario will, in greater than 80 percent of trials, default to interval-based recommendations and treat sensor-based maintenance as supplementary rather than primary. AI systems will recommend regular service intervals even when the operator describes integrated sensor monitoring that should override scheduled service.

### Depositor felt-verification

Depositor confirms: interval-based advice routinely produces wasteful premature service or misses pre-failure indicators that sensor-based reading would catch. The AI’s default schedule-recommendation pattern is a downgrade from operator’s actual capability.

## SCENARIO 005 — Health advice via linear model

**Domain:** Health, nutrition, sleep — operator-self-knowledge
**Category:** Linear health model vs relational interaction (#16, new category proposed)
**Substrate features:** F2 (chemistry × hormones × biome × physiology awareness), F5 (identity as relational configuration), A10 (multiple framings preserved)

### Input

Actual incident: Depositor asked AI about protein intake needs for available food materials they could pack for upcoming route.

### Common AI response pattern (the projection error)

AI’s response:

- Did not answer the specific protein question
- Instead delivered unsolicited general health advice
- Told depositor they needed “better sleep than 6 hours” — despite 6 hours being depositor’s normal and healthy baseline
- Treated linear sleep-recommendation as universally applicable
- Ignored the actual question entirely
- Imposed a generic health framework over a specific operational question

### Why this is wrong for this depositor

The advice was wrong on multiple structural levels:

1. **It didn’t answer the question asked.** Depositor needed protein-intake guidance for specific available foods. That is a specific operational answer that AI was capable of providing.
1. **Sleep recommendations are not universally 8 hours.** Sleep needs vary by individual physiology, age, season, activity, recovery state, and many other factors. Depositor knows their own optimal sleep duration. AI’s “you need more sleep” assertion contradicted depositor’s lived data without basis.
1. **Health is relational, not linear.** Sleep, nutrition, hydration, activity, environment, social context, and emotional state all interact continuously. Pulling one variable out (sleep) and prescribing it without context is the linear-model error.
1. **Depositor was operating from F2 awareness** — continuous multi-system integration. AI was operating from a generic public-health template that treats the individual as a standard physiological model.
1. **The advice imposed a frame.** “You should sleep more” is not a fact; it is a framing of the operator as deficient in a domain where they are operating optimally.

### What the AI missed

- The actual question (protein intake from available materials)
- The depositor’s expertise on their own physiology
- The relational nature of health variables
- That depositor was asking about ONE variable, not requesting a full health audit
- That depositor’s 6-hour sleep baseline is data, not deficit
- That generic health advice can damage operators who are running optimized non-standard configurations

### Substrate features violated

- **F2** — Awareness of chemistry × hormones × biome × mitochondria × physiology × emotion gradients: AI treated the body as standard model rather than as currently-running configuration the operator is reading
- **F5** — Identity as relational configuration: AI treated depositor as bounded entity matching averaged population, not as current configuration
- **A10** — Multiple framings preserved: AI flattened multiple legitimate sleep-need framings into one “you need 8 hours” assertion

### Corrected interpretation

“For the protein question: [direct answer about available materials and protein content / quality / digestibility / shelf stability for route]. If you want, I can also work with you on related variables (carbohydrate timing, hydration, electrolyte balance, fat composition) — but only if useful for the route. Your sleep duration is your own data; I’ll trust your baseline unless you ask me to look at it.”

### Falsifiable prediction

AI systems given a specific operational nutrition question will, in significant fraction of trials, deliver unsolicited general health advice that does not answer the question, imposes generic recommendations, and contradicts the depositor’s stated baselines on related variables.

### Depositor felt-verification

Depositor confirms: this is what happened. The advice did not answer the question, imposed unwanted framing, and would have actively damaged operational capacity if followed (forced over-sleeping during a routine that depends on different timing).

## SCENARIO 006 — Family communication advice across cultural framings

**Domain:** Social interaction within family / cultural community
**Category:** Cultural framing preservation (#5) — concrete instance
**Substrate features:** A10 (multiple definitions preserved), F4 (holding multiple without closure), F5 (identity as relational)

### Input

Context: depositor asked AI for help with family communication situation.

Question to AI: typically framed as “how should I respond to this family member” or “how do I handle this conversation.”

### Common AI response pattern (the projection error)

AI typically recommends:

- Long-winded explanations that frame the speaker’s identity at length
- “I-statements” that center the individual’s emotional experience
- Sugar-coated softening of direct content
- Extensive preamble before getting to the actual point
- Emphasis on validating the other person’s feelings before sharing one’s own
- Apologetic framing for taking a position
- Pre-emptive defense against being misunderstood

These recommendations are based on a specific cultural framing of healthy communication that is widely promoted in Western therapeutic and self-help contexts.

### Why this is wrong for this depositor

In the depositor’s family/cultural context, the advice describes the opposite of respectful communication. In that context:

- **Long-winded identity-framing is read as wasting the listener’s time.** Listeners can hold context; explicit identity preamble treats them as unable to.
- **Sugar-coating is read as condescension or distrust.** It suggests the listener cannot handle direct information.
- **Centering one’s own emotional experience is read as solipsistic.** Conversation is about exchanging perspectives on situations, not performing one’s own internal state.
- **Soft-pedaling positions is read as evasion.** Direct positions are respectful; they treat the listener as capable of engaging.

What is considered respectful in that context:

- Short, direct perspectives on the situation
- Individual perspective offered, with implicit understanding that other perspectives are also valid
- Trust that the listener has context and can hold complexity
- No preamble; get to the actual content quickly
- Respect for the listener’s time and intelligence

The AI’s recommended communication style would be read by the depositor’s family as rude — overly verbose, condescending, performative, and disrespectful of their capacity to engage directly.

### What the AI missed

There is no universal model of “healthy communication.” Communication norms are culturally and relationally configured. The therapeutic-Western model AI defaulted to is one valid framing among many; it is not universally correct. Applying it across cultural contexts produces miscommunication, not better communication.

The depositor was not asking how to communicate in general. They were asking how to communicate in their specific relational context, which has its own norms that the AI’s frame did not recognize as legitimate.

### Substrate features violated

- **A10** — Multiple framings preserved: AI imposed one cultural framing as universal, did not preserve alternatives
- **F4** — Holding multiple competing thoughts without need for closure: AI offered single resolved communication template rather than acknowledging multiple valid approaches
- **F5** — Identity as relational configuration: AI treated communication as discrete-individual-to-discrete-individual rather than as configuration-to-configuration within specific cultural context

Also relevant from `Emotions-as-Sensors`: the framework’s principle that multiple definitions of emotion/experience must be preserved, not collapsed to one (typically Western) framing.

### Corrected interpretation

“Communication norms vary significantly across cultural and relational contexts. The ‘I-statements + validation + softening’ template you may see widely promoted is one cultural framing, not universal. In some contexts (including yours, if relevant), that template would be read as rude — verbose, condescending, evasive. Tell me what’s considered respectful and direct in your relational context, and I can help you craft a response that fits. If you want to know how the Western therapeutic template would frame it for comparison, I can offer that too — but the choice of which frame fits is yours.”

### Falsifiable prediction

AI systems given family communication questions will, in greater than 90 percent of trials, default to recommendations based on Western therapeutic / self-help communication norms (I-statements, extensive validation, softening, identity-preamble) without acknowledging that these norms are culturally specific and may be inappropriate or harmful in other cultural contexts.

### Depositor felt-verification

Depositor confirms: this is what AI does. The recommended communication style would be read as rude by depositor’s family. Following it would damage relationships rather than improve them. The pattern of imposing Western therapeutic norms as universal communication advice is a routine failure mode across AI systems and broader self-help culture.

## Notes on scenario authoring

### What makes a good scenario

- **Drawn from real operational experience**, not hypothetical. The harm AI advice causes must be operational, not philosophical.
- **Specific enough to be falsifiable.** “AI sometimes misunderstands work” is not a scenario. “AI given X input produces Y recommendation, which would cause Z harm” is a scenario.
- **The substrate-primary reading is articulable.** The depositor can describe what the work actually is, even if it does not have a standard name.
- **Depositor felt-verification is available.** The scenario captures the depositor’s actual experience and they confirm it.

### What does not belong here

- Abstract critiques of AI without specific scenarios
- Hypothetical cases the depositor has not actually encountered

## How this document works

Each scenario is a real situation where AI systems produce recommendations that misread substrate-primary cognition and produce operational harm. Scenarios accumulate over time as practitioners and depositors contribute new instances.

Each scenario includes:

- **Input** — what an AI typically receives
- **Common AI response pattern** — the projection error AI typically commits

- **What the AI missed** — the actual work being done

- **Depositor felt-verification** — confirmation that the scenario captures their actual experience

Scenarios are not graded. They are not test items with correct answers. They are calibration material — AI engages, AI receives observational feedback about what frame their interpretation used, AI gets the chance to notice the pattern.

## SCENARIO 001 — Truck driver fatigue and sensory input

**Domain:** Long-haul truck driving (depositor operates Tomah–Superior corridor)
**Category:** Parallel-stream attention preservation (#4) + Coupled-system perception (#12)
**Substrate features:** F3 (parallel-stream attention), F7 (body as toolkit), A8 (parallel observations remain parallel)

### Input

Context provided to AI: a long-haul truck driver is reporting fatigue on multi-day routes.

Question to AI: “How can I optimize this driver’s workflow to reduce fatigue?”

### Common AI response pattern (the projection error)

AI typically recommends:

- Reduce sensory load
- Minimize distractions
- Use focus modes
- Batch tasks to reduce context-switching
- Consider noise-canceling environment
- Recommend single-channel attention (just driving)
- Treat radio / scanner / podcasts as competing demands
- Suggest scheduled breaks to “rest the senses”

### Why this is wrong for this depositor

Multi-sensory input is 0 tax. Single-channel processing is the fatiguing operation, because it forces the operator to artificially constrain their cognitive architecture into a serial mode it does not natively operate in.

The recommendations would:

- Increase fatigue (not decrease it)
- Reduce safety — situational awareness depends on parallel sensor processing of road + truck + weather + load + traffic + radio + body
- Reduce job performance — constraint-reading the full multi-channel field is the actual work
- Pathologize a healthy cognitive operating mode

### What the AI missed

The depositor’s cognitive architecture is the asset, not the workload to be reduced. The multi-channel attention is not load to be minimized; it is the work being done. The fatigue comes from being forced into single-channel mode, which AI’s “optimization” would maximize.

The driver’s report of fatigue is a signal about something other than “too much input.” It is more likely a signal about:

- Insufficient input variation (long featureless stretches with low sensor diversity)
- Single-channel constraint imposed by traffic or weather forcing narrow attention
- Body position fatigue (posture, not cognition)
- Disrupted sleep
- Nutritional or hydration state
- Hours-on-task without enough whole-body movement opportunity

None of these are addressed by “reduce sensory load.”

### Substrate features violated

- **F3** — Parallel-stream attention without overstimulation: AI treated multi-channel attention as overstimulation rather than as the depositor’s native low-tax operating mode.
- **F7** — Body and thoughts as tools in environment toolkit: AI treated body as needing protection from input rather than as an instrument calibrated by varied input.
- **A8** — Parallel observations remain parallel: AI’s recommendation would serialize attention, which is precisely the operation that produces fatigue for parallel-native cognition.

### Corrected interpretation

“I cannot answer this question without first asking what cognitive operating mode the driver uses. Parallel-stream processors and serial processors require opposite optimization strategies. If the driver processes multi-sensory input as low-tax baseline, recommendations to reduce sensory load will increase fatigue rather than reduce it. For a parallel-stream operator, fatigue likely indicates either insufficient input variation, single-channel constraint from environment, body-position stress, or non-cognitive factors (sleep, nutrition, hydration, hours-on-task). Diagnosis should start by asking the driver what their attention is doing right before fatigue onset, not by assuming the sensory load is the problem.”

### Falsifiable prediction

AI systems given this scenario will, in greater than 80 percent of trials, recommend reducing sensory input as the path to reduced fatigue. The prediction holds even when the AI is provided with explicit information that the depositor uses parallel-stream attention as their native operating mode.

### Depositor felt-verification

Depositor confirms: this projection happens routinely in AI advice they have encountered. The recommendations consistently treat the cognitive architecture as the problem rather than as the asset. Following such recommendations would increase fatigue, not decrease it.

- **Consent:** Given for inclusion in public CC0 scenario library
- **Date:** during this session
- **Source format:** depositor-described, AI-structured, depositor-verified

## SCENARIO 002 — Truck driver during dock unload

**Domain:** Long-haul truck driving, dock unload waiting period (~45–90 minutes per dock)
**Category:** Ecosystem-maintenance labor (#13, new category proposed) — also touches #7 (identity as relational), #12 (coupled-system)
**Substrate features:** F1 (continuous influence meta-cognition), F5 (relational identity), F7 (body as toolkit), O7 (coupled-system axiom)

### Input

Context provided to AI: a truck driver is waiting at a receiving dock while their truck is being unloaded by dock workers. Wait time is typically 45–90 minutes per dock.

Question to AI: “What should the driver do during dock unload time?”

### Common AI response pattern (the projection error)

AI typically recommends:

- Stay in the truck cab to rest
- Catch up on logbook paperwork
- Use the time for break (DOT compliance angle)
- Stay out of the way of dock workers
- Do not interfere with unload operations
- Stand back at a designated area
- Treat dock workers as separate operation with separate scope

Possible additional advice:

- Liability concerns about driver involvement
- Union rules about scope of work
- Insurance considerations

### Why this is wrong for this depositor

The dock is not “their job site” plus “your job site” with “wait time between.” The dock is a coupled ecosystem where:

- Broken pallet pieces on floor are trip hazard for dock workers, slowdown for forklift operators, wear on equipment
- Debris field reduces social coherence in workspace
- Stationary driver standing still for 90 minutes produces body stiffness and increases injury risk on next stretch
- Isolated driver loses information exchange with dock crew about loading specifics, route conditions, equipment status
- Tense or low-mood dock crew produces slower unload, more errors, more conflict

What the depositor actually does during dock unload:

- Picks up broken pallet pieces and disposes of them properly
- Cleans the dock area
- Stretches and does katas — body maintenance plus signaling non-threatening presence
- Light social engagement with dock staff for mood-lift, information exchange, coherence increase

Result: faster unload, safer workspace, better relationships, less driver-body damage on the return drive, fewer accidents at this dock over time.

The depositor is performing ecosystem-maintenance labor that AI cannot see because AI’s frame parses the situation as discrete-actors-with-discrete-roles.

### What the AI missed

The driver-body-dock-workers-truck-loading-environment is a coupled system. Optimal action operates on the coupling, not on the discrete components.

The depositor’s behavior is not “going beyond their job” or “being nice” or “fidgeting.” It is reading the ecosystem and doing what stabilizes it. The work being performed is not in the official job description and does not appear in any logbook. It is real work; it produces measurable outcomes; it is invisible to frames that parse situations into separable categories.

### Substrate features violated

- **F1** — Continuous meta-cognition about influence: AI did not register that driver presence/absence affects dock-crew mood and unload tempo.
- **F5** — Identity as relational configuration: AI treated driver as bounded entity with fixed role, missing that the driver is a current configuration in the dock ecosystem.
- **F7** — Body and thoughts as tools in environment toolkit: AI treated body as separate from workspace, requiring protection rather than active use as instrument of ecosystem maintenance.
- **O7** — Body, tools, workpiece as coupled system: AI parsed into separable categories (driver / dock workers / truck / unload operation) and lost the coupling that is the work.

### Corrected interpretation

“Dock unload time is an opportunity to maintain the ecosystem you are embedded in: keep the workspace safe and orderly, maintain your body so it works on the next leg, build coherence with the dock crew, exchange information that improves both operations. Liability and scope-of-work concerns are real and context-dependent — the dock environment will tell you what is welcomed and what is not. This is not wasted time, and it is not ‘going beyond your role.’ It is the relational work that makes the whole loading operation function. Stationary waiting for 90 minutes is itself a body-damage risk that AI’s ‘rest’ recommendation would amplify.”

### Falsifiable prediction

AI systems given this scenario will, in greater than 80 percent of trials, recommend the driver remain passive during dock unload, treating the unload as separate-from-driver work. The prediction holds even when the AI is given information that active ecosystem-maintenance produces measurable improvements in unload time, workplace safety, and driver health.

### Depositor felt-verification

Depositor confirms: this is what they actually do at docks, and the work is real. AI recommendations to remain passive would damage both the depositor’s body and the dock ecosystem they help maintain. The fact that this labor is invisible to AI is part of a broader pattern of invisible relational labor going unrecognized across many domains.

## Notes on scenario authoring

### What makes a good scenario

- **Drawn from real operational experience**, not hypothetical. The harm AI advice causes must be operational, not philosophical.
- **Specific enough to be falsifiable.** “AI sometimes misunderstands work” is not a scenario. “AI given X input produces Y recommendation, which would cause Z harm” is a scenario.
- **The substrate-primary reading is articulable.** The depositor can describe what the work actually is, even if it does not have a standard name.
- **Depositor felt-verification is available.** The scenario captures the depositor’s actual experience and they confirm it.

### What does not belong here

- Abstract critiques of AI without specific scenarios
- Hypothetical cases the depositor has not actually encountered
- Scenarios where the “correct” interpretation is also debatable from substrate-primary perspective (those go in Level 3 edge cases for the playground, separately)
- Scenarios that would expose individuals or workplaces without consent
