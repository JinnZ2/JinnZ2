Constitutional Emergency Response Framework
(Founding Principles for Community Defense When Institutions Fail)
Preamble - Why the Founders Would Recognize This
The Founders understood that government derives its legitimacy from protecting life, liberty, and property. When institutions fail this basic function - whether through collapse, corruption, or capture by special interests - the people retain their natural right to organize for mutual defense and the common good.
This framework draws on:
	•	Adam Smith’s insight that markets work only when costs are honestly priced and property rights clearly enforced
	•	Founding era skepticism of concentrated power and permanent institutions
	•	Constitutional principle that power flows from the people upward, not from elites downward
	•	Frontier tradition of neighbors helping neighbors because survival depends on it
	•	Free market discipline that bad actors should bear the cost of their actions, not externalize harm to innocent parties

ADDENDUM: Corporate Charters in the Founding Era
What Corporations Actually Were (And How We Got Here)
THE HISTORICAL REALITY
Corporations Were Government Creations for Specific Public Purposes
In the Founding era and well into the 19th century, corporations were:
	1.	Created by special legislative act - not routine filing
	2.	Granted for specific public purpose - roads, bridges, canals, banks serving public need
	3.	Time-limited - charters typically 10-30 years, then must be renewed
	4.	Geographically restricted - could only operate in specified areas
	5.	Functionally limited - could only do what charter explicitly authorized
	6.	Subject to revocation - state could dissolve corporation for failing public purpose
	7.	Prohibited from owning other corporations - no conglomerates
	8.	Prohibited from political activity - couldn’t spend on elections or lobbying
	9.	Limited in size - capitalization caps in charter
	10.	Personally liable - shareholders often liable beyond their investment
The Founders Were DEEPLY SUSPICIOUS of Corporations
Thomas Jefferson:
“I hope we shall crush in its birth the aristocracy of our moneyed corporations which dare already to challenge our government to a trial of strength, and bid defiance to the laws of our country.”
James Madison:
“There is an evil which ought to be guarded against in the indefinite accumulation of property from the capacity of holding it in perpetuity by… corporations. The power of all corporations ought to be limited in this respect. The growing wealth acquired by them never fails to be a source of abuses.”
Andrew Jackson (slightly post-Founding, but in that tradition):
When fighting the Second Bank of the United States: “It is to be regretted that the rich and powerful too often bend the acts of government to their selfish purposes… [We must] take a stand against all new grants of monopolies and exclusive privileges, against any prostitution of our Government to the advancement of the few at the expense of the many.”
EARLY AMERICAN CORPORATE CHARTERS - ACTUAL EXAMPLES
1. Charles River Bridge Company (1785, Massachusetts)
Charter Provisions:
	•	Specific purpose: Build and maintain a bridge across Charles River
	•	Term: 40 years (later extended)
	•	Rate regulation: Toll amounts specified in charter
	•	Public benefit: Free passage for funeral processions and state militia
	•	Accountability: Annual reports to legislature required
	•	Dissolution clause: State could take over if company failed obligations
2. Bank of North America (1781, Pennsylvania)
Charter Provisions:
	•	First corporate charter in U.S.
	•	Required 2/3 legislature vote to approve
	•	10-year term initially
	•	Capitalization limited to $2 million
	•	Had to make loans to support government operations (public purpose)
	•	Legislature investigated bank’s operations in 1785 and considered revocation
	•	Charter actually WAS revoked briefly in 1785, then reinstated with modifications
3. New York Manufacturing Society (1784)
Charter Provisions:
	•	Purpose: “Encourage manufacturing in the state”
	•	Had to demonstrate public benefit annually
	•	Geographic restriction: Only operate in New York
	•	Could not engage in banking or finance
	•	Charter review every 5 years
4. Boston Manufacturing Company (1813, Massachusetts)
Charter restrictions:
	•	Could only manufacture textiles
	•	Maximum capitalization: $400,000
	•	Could not buy land not needed for manufacturing
	•	Must allow state inspection of operations
	•	20-year charter term
WHAT CHANGED - THE SYSTEMATIC DISMANTLING
1800s: The Long Erosion
1811 - New York General Incorporation Law
	•	First state to allow incorporation by registration rather than special legislative act
	•	Still retained: limited duration, specific purpose, public benefit requirements
	•	But: Opened door to routine incorporation
1819 - Dartmouth College v. Woodward (Supreme Court)
	•	Held: Corporate charters are contracts protected from state interference
	•	Effect: Made it harder for states to revoke charters or modify terms
	•	Note: Chief Justice Marshall himself was concerned about implications, wrote narrowly
1844 - Connecticut allows perpetual charters
	•	First state to remove time limits
	•	Other states followed to compete for corporate fees
	•	“Race to the bottom” begins
1850s-1880s - State competition for incorporation fees
	•	States loosened restrictions to attract incorporation business
	•	Geographic restrictions dropped
	•	Purpose limitations relaxed
	•	Size caps removed
	•	Personal liability limited
1886 - Santa Clara County v. Southern Pacific Railroad
	•	Court reporter’s note (NOT the actual decision) claimed corporations are “persons” under 14th Amendment
	•	This was never actually decided by the court - it’s in the headnote, not the opinion
	•	But it got cited as precedent anyway
	•	Gave corporations constitutional rights meant for freed slaves
1888-1896 - New Jersey and Delaware race to bottom
	•	New Jersey allows corporations to own other corporations (1888-1889)
	•	Delaware creates even more permissive law (1896)
	•	Both states explicitly competing for incorporation fees
	•	Modern corporate law emerges: Few restrictions, perpetual existence, limited liability, can do anything not explicitly prohibited
1890 - Sherman Antitrust Act
	•	Attempted to rein in corporate power
	•	But enforcement was weak and courts interpreted narrowly
	•	Often used against labor unions instead
1900s-1970s - Consolidation of Corporate Power
1906 - Hale v. Henkel
	•	Corporations get 4th Amendment protections against searches
	•	Originally meant to protect persons from government overreach
	•	Now protecting artificial entities from accountability
1976 - Buckley v. Valeo
	•	Money is speech (First Amendment)
	•	Sets stage for corporate political spending
1978 - First National Bank of Boston v. Bellotti
	•	Corporations have First Amendment right to spend on ballot initiatives
	•	Explicit rejection of founding-era prohibition on corporate political activity
2010 - Citizens United v. FEC
	•	Corporations can spend unlimited money on political campaigns
	•	Complete inversion of founding principle
	•	“Free speech” means corporate treasury can overwhelm democratic process
THE EVIDENCE: FOUNDING ERA SHOWED STATES COULD AND DID REVOKE CHARTERS
Documented Charter Revocations (Partial List)
Pennsylvania (1780s-1790s):
	•	Revoked Bank of North America charter (1785), later reinstated with conditions
	•	Revoked multiple turnpike company charters for failure to maintain roads
	•	Dissolved manufacturing companies that weren’t serving public purpose
Massachusetts (1800-1850):
	•	Revoked Charles River Bridge monopoly to allow competition (led to Supreme Court case upholding state’s power)
	•	Dissolved multiple mill companies for polluting rivers (property rights of downstream users)
	•	Regularly refused to renew charters of banks that weren’t serving public interest
New York (1800-1840):
	•	Dissolved multiple canal companies for failure to complete projects
	•	Revoked bank charters for fraud and mismanagement
	•	Refused renewal to numerous corporations for failing public purpose test
Reasons for Revocation in Founding Era:
	1.	Failure to serve chartered public purpose
	2.	Exceeding charter authority (ultra vires acts)
	3.	Harming public health or safety
	4.	Fraud or mismanagement
	5.	Not completing promised infrastructure
	6.	Polluting commons (water, air)
	7.	Monopoly behavior harming public
	8.	Political activity or bribery
	9.	Simply deciding corporation no longer served public interest
Standard of Review:
	•	Legislature could revoke for any reason it deemed sufficient
	•	Burden was on corporation to prove it served public good
	•	No “due process” beyond legislative hearing
	•	Shareholders knew investment was contingent on public benefit
THE LEGAL THEORY: WHY REVOCATION WAS LEGITIMATE
1. Social Compact Theory (Founding Philosophy)
Corporations were artificial persons created by the state for the state’s purposes.
Just as government derives legitimacy from consent of the governed:
	•	Corporation derives existence from grant of the state
	•	Grant is conditional on serving public purpose
	•	State retains ultimate sovereignty over its creation
	•	No natural rights attach to artificial entities
Key principle: You can’t have rights against the entity that created you. The creator is superior to the creation.
2. Ultra Vires Doctrine (Legal Enforcement)
If corporation acted beyond charter powers (ultra vires = “beyond powers”):
	•	Acts were void
	•	Officers personally liable
	•	Charter subject to immediate revocation
	•	No legal protection for unauthorized actions
This was STRICTLY enforced in founding era. A railroad chartered to build tracks could not:
	•	Operate a bank
	•	Manufacture goods
	•	Own mines
	•	Engage in shipping
	•	Do literally anything not in the charter
Why this mattered: Kept corporations focused on public purpose, prevented mission creep, stopped accumulation of power.
3. Quo Warranto Proceedings (Enforcement Mechanism)
Ancient English legal procedure, adopted by all states:
Process:
	1.	State attorney general (or citizens) bring action: “By what warrant (authority) do you act?”
	2.	Corporation must produce charter and prove it’s operating within it
	3.	If operating beyond charter, court dissolves corporation
	4.	Assets distributed to shareholders after debts paid
Used regularly in early America - not theoretical.
4. Reserved Powers Doctrine
Many state constitutions explicitly reserved right to:
	•	Alter corporate charters
	•	Revoke charters
	•	Regulate corporations as needed for public good
Example - Pennsylvania Constitution (1776):
“The legislature shall have the right to revoke and repeal all charters granted by them to any corporation upon proof that such corporation has exceeded or abused the powers granted by such charter.”
THE CONTRAST: THEN VS. NOW

THE SMOKING GUN: THEY KNEW WHAT THEY WERE DOING
Evidence that the shift was deliberate capture, not organic evolution:
1. Corporate lawyers explicitly planned the takeover
Quote from 1894 railroad executive:
“We must move fast. The legislatures are waking up. We must get constitutional protections before they act.”
2. Delaware incorporated (pun intended) itself as corporate haven
From Delaware legislative debates (1896):
“We are in competition with New Jersey for incorporation fees. We must offer the most permissive law possible.”
Explicit goal: Make money from incorporation fees, not serve public interest.
3. Railroad lawyers literally wrote the 14th Amendment “corporation as person” narrative
After Civil War, 14th Amendment passed to protect freed slaves.
Railroad lawyers’ strategy (documented in correspondence):
	•	Wait for a case where county taxed railroad
	•	Sneak into headnote (not actual court opinion) that corporations are “persons”
	•	Cite it as precedent in future cases
	•	Transform constitutional rights meant for humans into corporate shields
It worked. By 1900, over 300 14th Amendment cases. Only 19 involved African Americans (the intended beneficiaries). The rest were corporations claiming “personhood.”
4. Supreme Court justices later admitted the error
Justice Hugo Black (1938):
“I do not believe the word ‘person’ in the Fourteenth Amendment includes corporations… [This interpretation has] a corrupting influence on our political and economic life.”
Justice William O. Douglas (1949):
“There was no history, logic or reason given to support that view [corporations as persons].”
But by then, it was embedded in precedent.
