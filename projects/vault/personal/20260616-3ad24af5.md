# The Shared Plan as a Manufacturing Contract

---

Marriage, the build plan, and the expectations attached to them map cleanly onto
a long-run manufacturing contract — the kind with tooling investment, milestones,
acceptance, and termination liability. The mapping is useful because the contract
world already has precise names for what happened, and the strongest of those
names **do not depend on proving intent.**

---

## 1. The mapping

| Contract element | Marriage / plan equivalent | What it means here |
|---|---|---|
| RFQ / vendor qualification | Courtship, getting to know each other | Mutual capability assessment before commitment |
| Spec / acceptance criteria | Expectations — what a working life together is | The definition of "delivered." Often implied, rarely written |
| The contract | Marriage + the tiny-house/land plan | Terms, deliverables, milestones, schedule |
| NRE / tooling investment | Upfront planning, permitting, design | Non-recurring engineering — paid once, before production |
| Capital account | The down-payment fund | Pooled capital earmarked for the build |
| Relationship-specific assets | Windows, appliances, materials for *this* site | Tooling with little value outside this one contract |
| WIP inventory | Daily care, provision, scheduling, labor | Work-in-process accumulating toward the deliverable |
| Progress milestones | Anniversaries, the land closing | Points where completed work is recognized |
| Change order | Renegotiating scope as life shifts | Legitimate if mutual; fraudulent if backdated |
| Acceptance / inspection | Continuing to accept care and effort | Acceptance by conduct — you take delivery, you accept |
| Termination | "I need space" + fund seizure | Walking the job mid-contract |
| Salvage / disposition | Stranded materials and labor | Who owns the tooling, who eats the WIP write-off |
| Final settlement | Reconciliation of who owes what | Closeout accounting |

## 2. The three concepts that do the real work

**Asset specificity.** Some investments only have value inside one specific
contract — tooling cut for one part, materials bought for one site, years of
provision aimed at one shared future. Outside that contract they're near-worthless.
The windows and appliances "for both," the planning, the daily care: relationship-
specific assets. High specificity is what makes the next concept possible.

**The hold-up problem.** This is the named economic failure, not a metaphor. Once
one party has sunk specific investments, the other party can capture their value by
threatening to walk — because the investing party now has far more to lose from
collapse than the exiting party does. The exit threat lets the holder appropriate
the "quasi-rents": the surplus value those specific investments created. Pooled
funds + accumulated provision on one side, exit leverage on the other, is the
textbook setup. The party who invested specifically is the one exposed to hold-up.

**Acceptance by conduct.** In a supply contract, if you take delivery of work over
a long period without rejecting it, you accept it — and you waive the right to
reject later on grounds you knew about all along. Years of accepting care, planning,
and provision is acceptance by conduct. A defect claim raised only *after* the
capital is secured, on grounds present the whole time, is a late rejection the
record already waived.

---

## 3. The failure mode: convenience dressed as cause

Contracts distinguish two terminations:

- **Termination for cause** — the other side breached; you exit and *they* owe you.
- **Termination for convenience** — you exit because you want to; permitted in
  many contracts, but it triggers **termination liability**: you pay for completed
  work, WIP, committed materials, and demobilization.

"I need space," then retroactive "doubts," is **termination for convenience being
re-papered as termination for cause** — manufacturing a defect after the fact so
the exit looks justified and the liability looks avoidable. The retroactive-doubt
move is, in contract terms, an attempt to convert a convenience termination (which
he pays for) into a cause termination (which he doesn't).

---

## 4. Why this frame is stronger than "breach"

Here is the part that matters most:

**Under either termination type, the terminating party owes the settlement.**

- For *cause*: requires proving the other side's bad conduct — hard, contested,
  motive-dependent.
- For *convenience*: requires proving **nothing about intent** — exit alone triggers
  liability for completed work, WIP, specific assets, and demobilization.

So the whole argument about whether his doubts were genuine or performed becomes
*unnecessary*. Even the most charitable reading — he simply changed his mind, no
bad faith at all — is **termination for convenience**, and convenience still owes:
the stranded specific assets, the WIP labor, the demobilization (the move), the
tooling write-off. You don't have to win the intent fight. You can concede it
entirely and the liability still attaches.

That's the gift of the manufacturing frame: it lets you drop the part of the case
that was unprovable and stand on the part that holds regardless.

## 5. What a complete contract model also includes (for rigor)

A real contract framework isn't one-directional, and naming the other terms makes
the strong terms above more defensible, not less:

- **Implied vs. written spec.** Many genuine disputes are spec ambiguity — both
  sides held different acceptance criteria, neither wrote them down. This can be
  honest failure rather than bad faith. It does not, however, erase termination
  liability; an unclear spec doesn't let you walk with the capital.
- **Mutual obligation.** A contract has two sets of deliverables. Naming your own
  obligations and showing they were met is what makes the acceptance-by-conduct
  argument airtight — he accepted *your* performance for years.
- **Good-faith renegotiation exists.** Scope can change by mutual change order.
  The line between a legitimate change order and a unilateral walk is *consent* —
  and consent is exactly what a unilateral fund seizure skips.

## One-line version

A long marriage with a shared build is a manufacturing contract heavy with
relationship-specific tooling. Exit after the capital is pooled is a classic
hold-up; relabeling it with backdated defects is convenience-termination wearing
a cause-termination mask. And convenience still owes — so the settlement stands
even if every doubt he claims is taken at face value.
